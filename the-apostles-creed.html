The apostles creed

The apostles creed and the heresies that rejected it

  1. What is the Apostles’ Creed?

The Apostles’ Creed is an early statement of Christian faith, summarizing essential Christian beliefs about God the Father, Jesus Christ, the Holy Spirit, and the Church.

It is shorter and simpler than the Nicene Creed.

Traditionally associated with the apostles’ teaching, but it was formally developed later.

It was primarily used in Baptism in the early Church.

  1. Historical Development

A. 1st–2nd Centuries

Early Christians recited baptismal formulas as a profession of faith.

These were oral summaries of apostolic teaching (Matthew 28:19; 1 Corinthians 15).

Early Church Fathers, such as Irenaeus (c. 130–202 AD), refer to short creeds used in baptism.

B. 3rd–4th Centuries

By the 3rd century, a more fixed version of a creed for baptism existed in Rome, called the Old Roman Creed.

It was used by local churches in Italy, Gaul, and the West.

This is considered the direct precursor of the Apostles’ Creed.

C. 5th–7th Centuries

The creed gradually expanded and standardized in the West.

By the 7th century, the version we now call the Apostles’ Creed was widely used in Western Christianity, especially for catechesis and baptism.

D. Medieval Period

It became the official baptismal creed in the Latin Church.

Used for teaching converts, especially children.

  1. Heresies that Rejected or Modified It

A. Gnosticism (2nd–3rd c.)

Some Gnostic groups denied Christ’s humanity or creation of the material world.

They rejected portions of the creed that spoke of Jesus’ birth, death, and resurrection.

Church response: The creed emphasizes Jesus’ incarnation, crucifixion, and resurrection as historical truths.

B. Arianism (4th c.)

Arians denied the full divinity of Christ.

The creed’s statement “born of the Virgin Mary… true God” directly countered this.

Church response: Local and ecumenical councils reaffirmed Christ’s divinity in creeds (Nicaea 325 AD, Constantinople 381 AD).

C. Manichaeans / Dualists (3rd–4th c.)

Rejected material creation as evil, thus denying God as creator.

Church response: Apostles’ Creed explicitly affirms God as creator of heaven and earth.

  1. Timeline Summary

Year / Century Event

1st c. Oral baptismal formulas reflecting apostolic teaching 2nd c. Church Fathers (Irenaeus, Tertullian) reference early creeds 3rd c. Old Roman Creed emerges in Italy as baptismal profession of faith 4th c. Arian heresy arises; local churches reinforce creed statements about Christ’s divinity 5th–7th c. Apostles’ Creed standardized in Western Church for catechesis and baptism Medieval Creed becomes official baptismal and teaching formula in the Latin Church

  1. Key Points

Purpose: Profession of faith, especially in Baptism.

Defended against heresies: Gnosticism, Arianism, Manichaeism, etc.

Church Response: Emphasized the historical truth of Christ’s life, divinity, resurrection, and God’s creation.

Relation to Nicene Creed: Apostles’ Creed is older and simpler; Nicene Creed was developed later to address doctrinal disputes like Arianism.
