Arianism Exposed

  1. What is Arianism?

Arianism was a Christian heresy in the 4th century, named after Arius (c. 256–336 AD), a priest from Alexandria, Egypt.

Its main claim: Jesus Christ is not fully God.

Arius taught that:

Jesus was a created being, made by God the Father.

There was a time when the Son did not exist.

Therefore, Jesus is divine but not equal to God the Father.

This directly challenged the Church’s teaching of the Trinity, which says Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are co-equal and consubstantial.

  1. Historical Context

Early 4th century (c. 318 AD): Arius’ teachings spread in Alexandria and beyond.

Many Christians were confused because Arius’ arguments seemed to preserve the oneness of God — he wanted to protect monotheism, but it distorted Christ’s divinity.

  1. Church Response

A. Council of Nicaea (325 AD)

Called by Emperor Constantine I to settle the dispute.

Resulted in the Nicene Creed, which explicitly affirmed:

Jesus Christ is “begotten, not made, consubstantial with the Father”.

He is true God from true God, eternal, not created.

Outcome: Arius’ teachings were condemned as heresy.

B. After Nicaea

Arius was excommunicated, but his followers (the Arians) persisted for decades, especially in the Eastern Roman Empire.

Several emperors supported Arianism at different times, causing political and theological conflict.

Finally, by the end of the 4th century, orthodox Nicene Christianity became dominant.

  1. Why It Was a Heresy

Arianism was considered heretical because it:

  1. Denied the full divinity of Christ, undermining salvation: only God can save humanity.

  2. Contradicted Scripture passages that affirm Christ’s divinity (e.g., John 1:1, Colossians 2:9).

  3. Threatened the unity of the Church and the doctrine of the Trinity.

  1. Legacy

Arianism influenced some Germanic tribes (Visigoths, Ostrogoths) during the 4th–6th centuries.

It was eventually eradicated in Western Christianity, but it highlights the early Church’s struggle to define orthodox belief.

✅ Key Summary:

Who: Arius, 4th-century priest.

What: Jesus is created, not fully God.

When: c. 318–336 AD, condemned at Council of Nicaea, 325 AD.

Why rejected: Denies Christ’s divinity, contradicts Scripture, threatens salvation and Church unity.
